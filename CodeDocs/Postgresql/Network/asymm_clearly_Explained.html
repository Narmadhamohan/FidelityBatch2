Asymmetric Encryption and RSA Algorithm

The choice of multiplication or division in asymmetric encryption isn't something Alice or Bob directly decides; it depends on the mathematical operations and algorithms used in the cryptographic system they choose (e.g., RSA). Here’s how it works:

Key Concept: Public and Private Key Functions
  • Encryption: Performed using the public key and typically involves some form of mathematical operation (e.g., exponentiation or multiplication).
  • Decryption: Performed using the private key and reverses the encryption operation (which might feel like division but is actually modular arithmetic in most cases).

Example with RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) Algorithm:

RSA is one of the most well-known asymmetric encryption algorithms. Here’s what happens behind the scenes:

Key Generation:

  • Alice generates her public key and private key using large prime numbers and mathematical operations (modular arithmetic).

Encryption:

  • Bob uses Alice’s public key to encrypt a message.
  • The encryption process involves raising the message to the power of a part of the public key and then performing modular multiplication.

Decryption:

  • Alice uses her private key to decrypt the message.
  • Decryption involves a reverse operation, which is similar to division but actually done using modular exponentiation.
Why Multiplication and Division Seem Involved:
  • While it looks like multiplication and division (as in the simplified example), in reality, most asymmetric algorithms rely on:
  • Modular arithmetic: Working with large numbers and calculating remainders after division by a prime number (called a modulus).
  • Exponentiation: Raising numbers to powers during encryption and reversing this during decryption using a special process.

Example of RSA Simplified:

  • Encryption: Bob might compute something like:
    • Ciphertext = (Messagepublic key) mod N
  • Where N is a product of two prime numbers.
  • Decryption: Alice will use her private key to reverse this:
    • Message = (Ciphertextprivate key) mod N
  • This ensures that only Alice, with her private key, can reverse the encryption done by Bob using the public key.
Conclusion:
  • Multiplication and division are simplified ways to explain the concepts, but the actual operations depend on the encryption algorithm.
  • In algorithms like RSA, modular exponentiation is used rather than simple multiplication or division.
  • Bob and Alice don’t manually choose the operation; it’s determined by the encryption algorithm they use (like RSA).
